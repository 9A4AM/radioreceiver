Radio Receiver

An application to listen to radio transmissions from your browser using a cheap RTL-SDR stick.

What is this

Radio Receiver is an HTML5 webpage that uses an RTL-SDR USB stick to digitize radio signals. Then, it demodulates those signals on your computer and plays the resulting audio through your computer's speakers or headphones.

Most RTL-SDR sticks let you receive signals in any frequency between 30 and 1700 MHz. This includes interesting radio bands like FM radio, marine radio, air band (airplanes and airports), weather radio, amateur radio, police and other emergency services, etc.

You can also listen to frequencies below 30 MHz using an upconverter or some sticks' "direct sampling mode". These frequencies include AM radio, shortwave radio, HF amateur radio, and numbers stations, among others.

Radio Receiver can demodulate stereo FM, AM, narrowband FM, USB, and LSB. It has adjustable bandwidth filters so you can also listen to Morse code transmissions or challenging, distant stations.

Compatible hardware and software

Radio Receiver was written to work with an RTL-2832U-based receiver with a R820T tuner chip (or compatible). This hardware configuration is a little dated, but we are planning to add support for newer sticks very soon.

How to use Radio Receiver

How SDR works

Traditionally, radios were built entirely out of hardware such as oscillators, filters, or mixers. All these components were designed to tune into a particular frequency band and demodulate a particular type of signal, and modifying a radio to receive a different signal could be hard or even impossible.

The modern approach to radio is “Software-Defined Radio” (SDR), in which the hardware doesn't do everything anymore; instead, it now does the minimum and leaves most of the processing to a computer running specialized software. SDR systems are flexible (changing the signals they can receive is as simple as changing their software) and cheap, because the same hardware designs can be used for many different types of radio signals.

Your RTL-SDR stick is a European Digital TV receiver. It contains a microprocessor that runs a program that demodulates radio signals, extracts a video stream, and sends it to your computer. However, it is possible to configure the stick so that, instead of decoding the signals, it will send them to your computer. Radio Receiver will then receive those signals and demodulate them in a JavaScript program on your browser.

Tuning, bandwidth, and sample rate

When you tune your RTL-SDR stick into a frequency, you don't only receive signals in that exact frequency. Instead, you receive signals in a “band” of frequencies centered on the one you tuned into. The difference between the lowest and highest frequencies in the band is the “bandwidth”.

As the RTL-SDR stick digitizes the signals in that frequency band, it will send “samples” to the computer. The number of samples it sends per second is the “sample rate”, which is mathematically tied to the bandwidth: 1 million samples per second give you 1 MHz of bandwidth, 2 MHz require 2 million samples per second, etc.

Higher sample rates give you larger bandwidths and more signals you can “see” at once; on the other hand, they require more processing power on your computer. With RTL-SDR sticks, the sample rate is adjustable, but Radio Receiver currently sets it to about 1 million samples per second, for 1 MHz of bandwidth.

The user interface

You can see the Radio Receiver interface in the figure below. There are three parts to it, from top to bottom: the spectrum scope, the waterfall, and the bottom bar.

The spectrum scope

The spectrum scope represents the frequency band that Radio Receiver is tuned into. The left side shows the lower frequencies and the right side shows higher frequencies. The line on the top shows which frequency corresponds to each part of the scope; in this example, they go from 93.4 MHz on the left to 94.4 MHz on the right, with 93.9 in the center.

The yellow line “dancing” in the scope shows the power of the radio signals that were received on each frequency: the higher it gets, the more power. By watching this line, you can see the radio signals that are present in the frequency band. In this example, you can see three powerful signals on 93.5, 93.9, and 94.3 MHz, and a weak signal on 94.1 MHz.

On the left, a decibels scale helps you gauge the relative power of the signals and the noise.

The waterfall

The spectrum scope shows you the signals that are being received right now, but the waterfall lets you see what signals were received in the past.

The waterfall scrolls down slowly, showing more recent signals on the top and less recent ones on the bottom. On each line, the intensity and color of each pixel corresponds to the power received for its frequency.

You can use the waterfall to see if there are regular transmissions on other neighboring frequencies, and their characteristics. With enough practice, you can even tell what kind of transmission it is, and you could even make a good guess at what they are saying!

The bottom bar

The bottom bar has two parts. The first one lets you adjust the decibel ranges for the spectrum scope and the waterfall. You can edit the values directly, or you can drag the two sliders to the right and left, closer together or farther apart.

As you drag the sliders, you will see changes in the dB scale on the left of the spectrum scope, and you will also see that the colors in the waterfall will become different to represent the new range.

The second part of the bottom bar lets you zoom in to see a particular part of the frequency band in more detail. You can click the magnifier with a “plus” sign to zoom in, or with the “minus” sign to zoom out, or you can type your zoom level directly in between them.

When you are zoomed in, you can scroll left and right using the scrollbar that appears on the right.
